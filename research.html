Research

Research Interest

My research interest centers on understanding language through a holistic and multi-disciplinary lens, bridging quantitative methods (such as neurolinguistics and NLP) with theoretical inquiry (including formal linguistics and the philosophy of language). Specifically, I want to explore how language interacts with other cognitive faculties, such as working memory and logical reasoning, or more broadly, thought, to better map its role in human cognition. Additionally, I’m deeply interested in the methodology of psychological sciences (behavioral or brain science), hoping to contribute to more rigorous and interpretable ways of conceptualizing research and interpreting results.

Sample Questions

Here are some questions - broad or narrow, formal or informal - that constantly occupy my mind:

  1. What's the relationship between language and thought? Can we think without language? Can mathmaticians or philosophers think without language? Do we think in language or think with the help of language?
  2. How does language interact with other core cognitive functions? Whether we think in or with the help of language, it must inteface dynamically with vision, memories, and executive control. I am deeply interested in mapping the functional role of language within this integrated cognitive architecture.
  3. When do we truly understand a sentence? We frequently experience moments where we 'comprehend every individual word but fail to grasp the full meaning when they are taken together', especially when encountering dense philosophical texts. What cognitive mechanisms account for this breakdown? I believe investigating this contrast offers a deeper paradigm for language comprehension than classical designs that merely contrast well-formed sentences with pseudo-words or non-words.
  4. LLMs resemble agents who are sensory-deprived from birth, learning symbols exclusively from textual statistics. How far can a system go in terms of genuine "understanding" based solely on linguistic input? Is it possible-as Derrida's "il n'y a pas de hors-texte" (there is no outside-text) or Heidegger's "language is the house of Being" might suggest-to construct a coherent model of the world purely out of language?
  5. How do we rapidly grasp a speech act? As John Searle noted, utilizing a performative word like "promise" presupposes a dense web of constitutive rules. Yet, in real-time communication, we do not painstakingly verify these prerequisites before speaking. What underlying computational or cognitive heuristics enable such high-speed pragmatic processing?
  6. What are the 'right' approaches to study questions in Psychological Sciences? While the pipeline of designing experiments, collecting data, and running statistical analyses is well-established, I argue that the most profound challenges lie in conceptualization and interpretation. How do we operationalize psychological constructs? Can we truly define them with the mathematical precision found in physics (e.g., mass, acceleration, velocity)? Furthermore, we must critically re-examine the coupling between methods and interpretation: how participant demographics, experimental paradigms, and analytic choices inherently constrain the correctness and generalizability of our theoretical conclusions.
